## Correction Analysis

### Task Response

**What was done well:**

- The position is stated immediately and clearly in the introduction ("I completely agree").
- Both parts of the question are addressed: part one (agree/disagree) in paragraphs 1–2, and part two (what type of punishment) in paragraph 3.
- The bullying example is specific and relevant.

**What needs improvement:**

- **Body paragraph 2 (why punishment works)** states that punishment "helps them realize their mistakes" but does not explain _how_ punishment leads to moral understanding. The reasoning jumps from example to conclusion without establishing the causal mechanism.
- **Body paragraph 3 (types of punishment)** gives two examples (pocket money, household chores) but only justifies them with "unlikely to hurt their feelings." A stronger answer would explain _why_ these specific measures are educationally effective — e.g., they create a proportionate consequence the child can understand.
- **The conclusion restates** rather than synthesises. "Bad behavior brings bad consequences" and "help them learn to tell right from wrong" have both already been stated in essentially identical words. This repetitiveness limits TR to Band 6.

### Coherence & Cohesion

**What was done well:**

- Four clear paragraphs with distinct functions (intro, argument for punishment, argument for mild punishment, conclusion).
- Several cohesive devices are used accurately: "Take, for example," "by contrast," "As a result," "In conclusion," "therefore."

**What needs improvement:**

- **Mechanical repetition at paragraph openings:** The introduction ends with "only mild punishments should be allowed," and paragraph 3 opens with "only mild punishments should be used." This near-identical phrasing signals a lack of cohesive progression rather than a development of ideas.
- **Within-paragraph flow in body 2:** The sentence "This leads them to believe that they do not need to respect their peers from low-income families" introduces a new sub-claim (class/income) that is not set up and is then abandoned. This disrupts the logical chain.
- **"However" overuse:** The word appears at the end of paragraph 1, the start of paragraph 3, and inside the conclusion — three times in a short essay, creating a mechanical feel.

## Band 7–9 Model Answer

Children who learn to distinguish right from wrong from an early age are better equipped to navigate social life responsibly. I fully agree that punishment is a necessary part of this moral development; however, **the form that punishment takes** is equally important, and only <span style="background-color: rgba(34, 197, 94, 0.26); color: inherit; border-bottom: 1px solid rgba(34, 197, 94, 0.68);"><strong>non-physical, proportionate measures</strong></span> should be permitted.

**When children engage in harmful behaviour without facing any consequences**, they receive no signal that their actions were unacceptable and are therefore likely to repeat them. **For instance**, a child who bullies classmates but encounters no disciplinary response will come to regard such behaviour as normal and permissible. **Punishment, in this context, serves a <span style="background-color: rgba(34, 197, 94, 0.26); color: inherit; border-bottom: 1px solid rgba(34, 197, 94, 0.68);">corrective function</span>**: it makes clear to the child that certain actions carry negative repercussions, <span style="background-color: rgba(34, 197, 94, 0.26); color: inherit; border-bottom: 1px solid rgba(34, 197, 94, 0.68);"><strong>thereby discouraging repetition</strong></span>. **Without this <span style="background-color: rgba(34, 197, 94, 0.26); color: inherit; border-bottom: 1px solid rgba(34, 197, 94, 0.68);">feedback mechanism</span>**, the boundary between acceptable and unacceptable conduct remains blurred, and children find it far harder to develop a reliable moral compass.

**That said**, the nature of the punishment matters enormously. **Harsh or physical punishments — such as spanking or severe scolding — risk provoking <span style="background-color: rgba(34, 197, 94, 0.26); color: inherit; border-bottom: 1px solid rgba(34, 197, 94, 0.68);">resentment and defiance</span>** rather than genuine reflection, particularly in young children who have yet to develop **the emotional maturity needed to process** criticism constructively. **By contrast**, **mild, proportionate measures** — such as withdrawing privileges, assigning additional household responsibilities, or implementing a short supervised cooling-off period — are far more likely to encourage children to consider the impact of their actions. **Crucially, any punishment should be accompanied by a calm explanation** of why the behaviour was wrong, <span style="background-color: rgba(34, 197, 94, 0.26); color: inherit; border-bottom: 1px solid rgba(34, 197, 94, 0.68);"><strong>so that children internalise the lesson rather than merely enduring the penalty</strong></span>.

In conclusion, **punishment is a legitimate and valuable tool** in teaching children to differentiate right from wrong, provided it is <span style="background-color: rgba(34, 197, 94, 0.26); color: inherit; border-bottom: 1px solid rgba(34, 197, 94, 0.68);"><strong>administered thoughtfully and proportionately</strong></span>. **Physical or humiliating punishments should be ruled out**; instead, parents and teachers should rely on <span style="background-color: rgba(34, 197, 94, 0.26); color: inherit; border-bottom: 1px solid rgba(34, 197, 94, 0.68);"><strong>measured, explanatory approaches</strong></span> that guide children toward moral understanding rather than simply deterring misconduct through fear.
