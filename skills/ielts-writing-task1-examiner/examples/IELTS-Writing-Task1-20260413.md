## Corrected Essay

The two pie charts illustrate the proportion of energy consumption in an average Australian household and the resulting greenhouse gas emissions from these activities.

Overall, it is clear that while heating consumes the largest share of energy, it produces a significantly smaller proportion of greenhouse gases. In contrast, "other appliances" and refrigeration contribute more to emissions relative to the amount of energy they use.

Regarding energy use, heating is the most significant factor, accounting for 42%, followed by water heating at 30%. Together, these two areas represent nearly three-quarters of total energy consumption. Other appliances ~~take up~~ `account for` 15%, while refrigeration, lighting, and cooling contribute much smaller amounts at 7%, 4%, and 2% respectively.

However, the greenhouse gas emissions show a different pattern. Water heating is the largest contributor here at 32%. Interestingly, "other appliances" ~~see~~ `show` a major jump, accounting for 28% of emissions — nearly double its energy use ~~percentage~~ `share`. Similarly, refrigeration's impact doubles to 14%. Conversely, even though heating is the top energy consumer, it only generates 15% of total emissions. Lighting and cooling remain the smallest contributors at 8% and 3%.

## Correction Analysis

### Task Achievement

**Well covered:**

- The overview paragraph correctly identifies both key contrasts: (1) heating's disproportionate energy-to-emissions ratio, and (2) other appliances and refrigeration showing the inverse pattern. This is precisely what examiners expect from a Band 7+ overview.
- All six categories are addressed in both charts with accurate figures.
- The structural choice to dedicate one body paragraph per chart and then make cross-chart comparisons within the second paragraph is effective.

**Missed or underdeveloped:**

- Lighting also nearly doubles from 4% (energy) to 8% (emissions) — a notable parallel to other appliances and refrigeration that goes unmentioned.
- The final sentence ("Lighting and cooling remain the smallest contributors at 8% and 3%") is factually correct but does not develop the comparison; noting that cooling's share barely changes (2% → 3%) while lighting doubles would add analytical depth.

### Coherence & Cohesion

No structural errors. Minor points:

- The sentence "Lighting and cooling remain the smallest contributors at 8% and 3%" feels slightly disconnected from the preceding analysis. A bridging phrase such as "In both charts, lighting and cooling remain the smallest contributors..." would improve cohesion.
- The discourse markers are varied and appropriate, but there is a slight over-reliance on single-word adverbial connectors ("Interestingly", "Similarly", "Conversely") — a subordinate clause connector ("Whereas heating accounts for the majority of energy use, its share of emissions is considerably smaller") would add further range.

## Band 7–9 Model Answer

The two pie charts compare **how energy is consumed** across different activities in a typical Australian household and the **corresponding share** of greenhouse gas emissions each activity generates.

Overall, the most striking feature is that heating, despite accounting for the largest portion of energy use, is responsible for a **disproportionately small share** of emissions. Conversely, water heating and other appliances produce emissions **well in excess of** their energy consumption proportions.

In terms of energy use, heating represents the dominant category at 42%, followed by water heating at 30%. Together, these two activities account for nearly three-quarters of total household energy consumption. Other appliances **constitute** 15%, whereas refrigeration, lighting, and cooling contribute considerably smaller shares of 7%, 4%, and 2% respectively.

**The emissions picture, however, differs markedly.** Water heating becomes the single largest emitter at 32%, while other appliances generate 28% of total emissions — almost double their energy consumption share. Refrigeration's contribution **likewise doubles** from 7% to 14%, and **lighting rises proportionally** from 4% to 8%. By contrast, heating accounts for only 15% of emissions **despite its dominant energy use**, and cooling remains the smallest contributor in both charts at 2% and 3% respectively.

### Structural Choices

1. **Overview placement:** The overview (second paragraph) immediately signals the main contrast before any data is introduced. This is the Band 7+ standard — the examiner can see you have grasped the data's meaning, not just its numbers.

2. **Data grouping:** Body paragraph 1 groups all energy use data together; body paragraph 2 addresses all emissions data. This mirrors your original structure, which is logical and easy to follow. The alternative — grouping by category (heating, then water heating, then appliances…) — risks feeling mechanical.

3. **Cross-chart observation in paragraph 2:** The model explicitly notes that other appliances "almost double their energy consumption share" and that refrigeration "likewise doubles" — this comparison technique is what distinguishes analytical description from mechanical listing.
